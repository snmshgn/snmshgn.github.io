Thematic

Overview

Tasked with creating an app from conception to design, I thought back on the problems I'd like to solve for myself. I decided that, as an avid reader, a recommendation app is the direction I wanted to go.

Problem

The question I wanted to answer was this: Is there a better way to find your next book to read?

I had always had some trouble finding books that covered overarching themes that I was interested in, and often those themes were not concentrated within a specific genre, which meant trying to find them became a guessing game of sorts. I asked this same question of my users during the research phase, finding that, yes, many people faced the same problem.

Solution

I decided to create Thematic, an app that allows a user to search for books based on genre, theme, or any cross-section of the two. It gives users information on what themes each book covers and has a recommendation feature based on previous books read, explicitly focusing on common themes relating to higher ratings.

User Research

Since I had chosen a concept that I myself had experience with, I chose to use that to inform my questions for user surveys and conversations with users on what kinds of things frustrated them, what they wanted, what they needed.

Users tended to fall into three categories:

  • Users who were specifically looking for inclusive storytelling,
  • users who were most concerned with the quality of writing, and
  • users who were more interested in what themes a book covered.

The most important common feature was the presence of other user reviews on a book. The most common frustration with existing products is the lack of a specified search option.

The user survey part of the process revealed that, unlike myself, most users did not consider themes to be more important than the genre they were currently reading. In fact, it was rather split in the middle, with 1/3 saying it didn’t matter more, 1/3 saying it sometimes did, and a final 1/3 saying that it mattered more.

Competitive Analysis

Our biggest competitor is GoodReads, but others included non traditional search rituals through amazon, google, wattpad, tumblr, twitter and other social networks.

While GoodReads markets itself specifically to book readers, others, such as Amazon Books, are a small part of a larger online commerce site, focused less on the user’s interest in which books they want to read, and more on what they should buy, which isn’t always the same thing.

I found two other competitors for the analysis, one of which I myself often used, and another that was new, but posed intriguing marketing for users. The first is BookRiot, a site dedicated to articles and news about books. It’s more of a blog than a recommendation site, but it’s a place I often start when searching, which made me think others might as well. The downside to BookRiot is the fact that they are articles, and not more specified information on specific books.

The second I found is called TBR: Tailored Book Recommendations. It markets itself as the StitchFix for bibliophiles. A read person reviews your answers to a quick survey, and sends you personally tailored recommendations based on those answers once a month or so. While the idea of this is very interesting, the downside is that it is a paid service, and that it sends a rather small selection of books to the user.

I figured a competitor had a chance to break into this market. The competition was rather sparse and divided, and there was an opportunity to create room by integrating some of their features into a new product.

Using this information, I developed a base idea for what my initial product would contain: The ability to search in specific ways, the ability to create and view user reviews, the ability to cross reference genres and themes, and to have a specified recommendation page working on the history of the user’s ratings and common threads.

User Stories

Using the information provided by my research, competitive analysis and user personas, I created a list of user stories that would be most pertinent to what users wanted to do with my app, particularly with the advanced search function. [User Stories]

User Flows & Sitemap

User flows were created with the user in mind, attempting to create sectional areas of the app that users could use for specific purposes. I faced some difficulty in breaking down the user flows to make them more legible, but kept the master file for it in tact as a guide for how all the disparate parts connect.

Even as I was creating the user flows, I knew there would be changes made to how content might be accessed. Given more time, I would have happily fleshed out the user stories more and added those to the user flows as a way to create a more cohesive base for the mockups. [User Flows]

The sitemap was based on the flows, condensing the actions into pages and links. The troubles with this were similar to those with the user flows, in that the file itself became so large it was difficult to navigate without breaking it down into smaller files and sections. [Sitemap]

Wireframes

Wireframing, and subsequent testing, created a couple different iterations of the app. Using preference testing along the way, as well as remote testing once all the pages were complete, I reworked and refined the lofi wireframes to reflect the changes needed for users.

Changes between iterations arose after testing and retesting with users through preference tests and small prototyping tests using Maze. Preference tests revealed more common UI patterns that users preferred for navigation, leading to less confusion about where they would click to get to where they wanted to go. The use of color in the hifi mockups also cleared up some usability confusion for testers since the way they were trying to navigate the page was by color and contrast, these being indicators for important information, what they can click, etc.

Design mockups

The mockups themselves went through about 4 different changes based on testing and retesting, both using preference tests and remote usability tests. In person testing revealed some other potential issues that led to more redesigning as well, eliminating unnecessary content for the user. This included getting rid of the language filter settings from the book recommendation settings page, as well as reworking the informational hierarchy of the account settings page. Every time a change was done or considered, either a preference test or a maze was launched to test the solution out.

The results showed that users often went to the image of a book to find the information on it. Making the touch target for the book view the entire area of the cover, title, author and description allowed users to easily navigate to where they wanted to go. Other things the testing revealed was the need for a home button on pages like the book view, author view and user view, otherwise users had trouble navigating back. Touch targets were initially a problem, as I was doing prototyping using the Sketch App prototype and Maze had trouble integrating all of the hotspots. It also made the touch targets the exact dimensions of the content within, meaning the text of a link was the touch target, so small that users had trouble accessing it. To fix this, I went back into the Sketch prototype and created padding areas that acted as the touch target of the content within. The padding for each of these areas was always 10px on each side of the content within.

Prototyping

We created a prototype using the low fidelity mockups, testing them with potential users to find flaws in the design. The initial design was clunky and didn’t allow users to view all parts of the track they had uploaded. Taking their considerations into account, we redesigned and retested.

Conclusion

The biggest takeaway from this project was the ability and necessity to go back and review our work at every step, to think of the viability of the product and continue to generate new solutions.

Pre-user survey research was found to be key as well. This would eliminate generalized thinking going forward and allow for more focused work and questions for testers.

The biggest doubt going into the project was the client brief itself. It was very vague, with almost no information on what exactly we ought to be making. There was no target audience, no clear idea of what kind of product we wanted other than a cloud based storage app, and no research on the client’s part for unique features and attributes. While this gave us a lot of options to pursue, it also put most of the work on the research team, relying on them to answer all the basic questions the client should have had some kind of idea of. The first read of the brief felt like the idea itself wasn’t viable without more work.

The biggest surprise was the different paths users found to accomplish a task. They opened new ways of thinking of navigation for us, and taught us that it is important to allow for multiple paths through a product.

Had we been given more time, we would have done far more research and testing on a larger audience. Finding musicians to test was difficult in the short period we were given and we would have benefitted from a larger base of testers.

The lessons learned in this project will carry with us through future designs, reminding us the importance of initial research and informal information for concept ideas.
